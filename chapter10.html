PART FIVE: THE TESTAMENT

Amara recorded one last message that night, in a tent on the edge of the excavation site.

"We didn't fight for a flag. We walked for a memory. For the idea that once, briefly, people tried to build something better."

"The walking is what makes us human. And the remembering is what makes us free."

The Disappearance

No one saw her leave. Her body was never found. No arrest was announced. No death reported.

PART SIX: THE BURIED SEED

Twenty years later, Isiba looked different again. The excavation site had been turned into a memorial. Sanitized. Commercialized.

The Wildflowers

Yellow. Stubborn. Persistent. Growing in concrete.

And occasionally, when no one was watching, someone would kneel beside the flowers and press their palm to the ground. Remembering.

EPILOGUE: THE QUESTION REMAINS

The covenant is in a museum now. The story is taught in schools. The dream is archived.

Because nations are not built once. They are built continuously. By every generation that chooses to remember.

Uganda is still walking.

REFLECTION

Can a dream survive its dreamers?

Does memory require victory, or just persistence?

When the march ends, does the walk continue?

If every generation must dig its own truth, is progress real or just the same struggle in new clothes?

And when the last person who remembers is gone, who will teach the children what we tried to build?

The seed was planted. Whether it grows depends on those who water it. The covenant is buried. Whether it's found depends on those brave enough to dig. The dream is sleeping. Whether it wakes depends on those stubborn enough to remember.

History is not written by victors. It is written by those who refuse to forget.

Uganda is still walking.
