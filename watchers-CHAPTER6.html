THE LAST THREE

Chapter Six

Part One: The Letter That Changed Everything

When Zeinab's final journal arrived in 1495, it came with something else.

A sealed container. Waterproof, marked with temporal radiation signatures that only the Watchers would recognize. Inside: a message from the future.

Not from 2187. From 2189. Two years after they had left.

"You need to know: we lost the return signal. The temporal anchor collapsed. We cannot bring you back. You cannot come home."

Part Three: The First Death

Tendai Moyo

1505 - Age 78

He had been inspecting the experimental drought-resistant millet when his heart simply stopped.

"You planted seeds for fifty-eight years. You taught them that survival starts with soil. That civilization grows from the ground up."

Part Four: The Years Between

Between 1505 and 1513, Amara and Jabari continued the work.

"We're not parents," Amara said. "Aren't we?" Jabari gestured at the university below. "We gave birth to this. We nurtured it. We watched it grow. We're letting it go. That's parenthood."

Part Five: The Second Death

Amara Okonkwo

1515 - Age 71

Amara's joints finally failed in 1515. Not slowly. Suddenly.

"Don't die alone. Wait until there's someone with you. Even if it's just a student. Even if it's just a stranger. Don't die with no one to witness."

Jabari lived for ten more years. Ten years of watching Mazingira grow. Ten years of teaching fewer classes. Ten years of loneliness.

Part Seven: The Final Entry

Three days later, Jabari felt it. The cellular collapse. The temporal displacement damage finally catching up.

His last thought was: *I hope this was enough.* His last breath was: *It has to be.*

Reflection: The Empty Sky

That night, after the tomb was sealed, Adanna stood where the Watchers had stood so many times‚Äîon the Observatory roof, watching the stars.

The Watchers hadn't prevented the catastrophe. But they had prepared people for it. They hadn't saved everyone. But they had saved something. They hadn't changed the ending. But they had changed the story.

The tomb was a bomb. A truth bomb. Waiting to explode across five centuries.

"It mattered," she whispered to the empty sky. "It mattered because you made sure it would."

Questions for Reflection

The Watchers learned they could never return home only after forty-eight years in the past. Does it matter that their sacrifice was forced rather than chosen? Or does the work matter more than the motivation?

Tendai wanted to turn their corpses into evidence‚Äî"the architecture of endings." Is there dignity in being found, studied, carbon-dated? Or is it just making yourself into an exhibit?

Jabari wrote "trying is the only thing that ever matters." But they tried and failed. Does failure negate the trying? Or does trying redeem the failure?

Amara spent forty-eight years asking "Does it matter?" and died still asking. Jabari said "Yes, because we tried." But is that a real answer or just something we tell ourselves to cope with meaninglessness?

The students sealed six corpses in a tomb full of journals and called it memory. But isn't it just a mass grave with paperwork? When does documentation become commemoration?

Jabari's last thought was "I hope this was enough." But enough for what? Prevention? Preservation? Peace? When is sacrifice ever enough?

The tomb is described as a "truth bomb waiting to explode across five centuries." But what if it's never found? What if it's found and ignored? What if truth isn't enough to change anything? Does that make the Watchers' deaths meaningless‚Äîor does meaning exist independent of results?

The Watchers were gone. But the watching continued.
