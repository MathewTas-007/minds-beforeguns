The Capital of Dust and Dreams

Chapter Five

The road curled toward the new capital like a scar made of stone. It stretched across the plateau — a ribbon of ambition connecting what had been divided, promising what had never been.

They called the place Isiba — "the ground where we agree."

PART ONE: THE GROUND WHERE WE AGREE

The Selection

The choice of location had taken the Council six months of brutal debate. Maps were spread across tables until their edges frayed. Elders walked the land with surveyors, their feet blistered and their patience thin.

"We are asking the wrong question," he said. "We are asking *which region deserves the capital*. But the question should be: *which place will force us to remain equals?*"

The Case for Karamoja

"Karamoja?" someone finally said. "There is nothing there but dust and cattle."

"Exactly," Sanyu replied. "Karamoja has been ignored by every government since the British drew the borders."

If we build our capital in Karamoja, we break that pattern. We say: *This nation is not built on what the colonizers gave us. It is built on what we choose to create.*

The Moral Argument

"If we build our capital in Karamoja, we cannot ignore them anymore. We cannot pretend they do not exist. Because the president will wake up every morning and see their children playing in the dust."

Because a nation that cannot confront its own neglect is not a nation. It is just a collection of comfortable lies.

PART TWO: THE FIRST STONE

At dawn, the founders gathered on the hill that overlooked the plain. The sun was red that morning — the kind that makes even a promise look suspicious.

"We build not to rule, but to reconcile."

PART THREE: THE CITY TAKES SHAPE

The Grid

"Cities are organic," Kalisa argued. "They grow around water, around markets, around gathering places. If you impose a grid on flat land, you create a city without a heart."

PART FOUR: THE VISITORS

Weeks turned into months, and foreign diplomats began arriving — tall, polished men in suits that smelled of air-conditioning and control.

Elder Sanyu called them *the harvesters who never plant.*

PART FIVE: THE PEOPLE ARRIVE

The Informal City

While the official city rose in straight lines and imported marble, a second city grew in the shadows. The informal city. The real city.

The planners called it "slums." The people called it "Isiba ya Kweli" — the real Isiba.

PART SIX: THE HOUSE OF THOUGHT

In the center of the official city, the Council commissioned a special building: The House of Thought.

"This is what the capital was meant for," Kalisa said. "Not buildings. Conversations."

PART SEVEN: THE FIRST ANNIVERSARY

On the city's first anniversary, they unveiled a monument — fifteen bronze figures, standing in a circle, holding a scroll between them.

Original Inscription:

"We build not to rule, but to reconcile."

New Inscription:

"We build to endure."

PART EIGHT: THE OTHER DREAM

Yet, each night, when the streetlights of Isiba flickered and the official city went to sleep, the people still gathered.

This was the nation being built. Not in marble. Not in speeches. But in the small, persistent acts of people who refused to let the dream die.

REFLECTION

Isiba still stands, half gold, half dust. From the air, it looks like progress. From the ground, it looks like compromise.

Because the people have learned a truth the planners never understood: A capital is not made of stone. It is made of memory. And memory, unlike marble, cannot be controlled.

CLOSING QUESTIONS

A road without thought becomes a march — but who decides when marching becomes trampling?

A city without soul becomes a fortress — but can a fortress ever feel like home?

When the monuments rise faster than the schools, what are we really building?

If the covenant is buried beneath the first stone, who will remember to dig it up?

And when the House of Thought is locked, where does the nation go to dream?

Isiba stands. But the question remains: is it a capital of the people, or just another city where the people happen to live?
