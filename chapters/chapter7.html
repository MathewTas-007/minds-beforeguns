Shadows Over Isiba

Chapter Seven

The drums of war did not sound when freedom died — they whispered.

Isiba, once a dream carved from red dust, now lay wrapped in fear.

PART ONE: THE YEARS OF SILENCE

The General's Portrait

In every government building, in every school, in every market square, a portrait hung. The general's face — stern, unsmiling, painted to watch from every angle.

"And slowly, people forgot that he was just a man. He became the uniform. The flag. The state itself."

PART TWO: THE NEW ORDER

The general ruled by decree. The Council was dissolved. The newspapers printed what they were told. The radio spoke with one voice.

To question was treason. To remember was rebellion.

The Division of Internal Harmony

DIH

Division of Internal Harmony

"Unity Through Vigilance."

Official Slogan

The people whispered: "Unity Through Fear."

PART THREE: THE HIDDEN NETWORK

But dreams, like seeds, do not die easily.

The Keepers

Teachers, poets, former clerks, market women

"Remember the covenant. Remember that nations die only when memories do."

The Underground Press

The Buried Truth

Single page, hand-cranked press

What They Published:

  • • Names of the disappeared
  • • Location of mass graves
  • • Terms of foreign loans
  • • The covenant's text

Distribution:

Market women hiding copies in vegetable baskets

PART FOUR: THE WAR FOR TRUTH

The Night of Long Knives

The Cleansing

"Over three nights, the DIH arrested two hundred people. Teachers. Poets. Market women. Students."

The people clapped, because they had forgotten how not to.

PART FIVE: THE LONG NIGHT

For years, the silence deepened. Children grew up believing that freedom had always worn a uniform.

"The dream is not dead. It sleeps."

Kalisa in Exile

Kalisa fled north, then east, then finally across the border into Kenya. She lived in a refugee camp, teaching children who had lost everything.

"Someday," she would say, "someone will dig it up. And when they do, they will understand."

And memory, in the end, is the only weapon that cannot be confiscated.

PART SIX: IF MEMORY SURVIVES

If nations die when they forget, then Uganda — though wounded — was not yet dead.

The long night continued — but the dawn was listening.

REFLECTION

Isiba still stands, but hollow. The marble is cracked. The fountains are dry. The House of Thought is a barracks. The streets are watched.

Yet somewhere, in basements and refugee camps, in whispered songs and underground newspapers, The Other Dream survives.

Can a nation survive when truth becomes treason?

When the only history taught is the victor's lie, who remembers what really happened?

If a covenant is buried and the map is lost, will anyone ever find it?

When fear replaces thought, can a people ever think freely again?

And when the general dies, who will inherit his silence — or his ghosts?

The tyrant can burn the books, silence the voices, and erase the names. But he cannot kill the stories told in the dark. Because stories, unlike statues, cannot be toppled. They simply wait.
