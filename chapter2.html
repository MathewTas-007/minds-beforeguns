The First Cracks in the Dream

Chapter Two

PART ONE: A FRAGILE FREEDOM

The morning after independence was painted with a thousand promises. Kampala's hills echoed with music, speeches, and banners that carried the words *freedom*, *unity*, *progress*. For the first time in decades, the people believed their destiny was in their own hands.

But destinies are not seized in a single night. They are built, brick by brick, over generations — and the first brick Uganda laid was already crooked.

The celebrations lasted three days. The streets were rivers of color — red, black, and yellow dancing on flags, on dresses, on the faces of children who had no memory of what it meant to be ruled by foreigners.

In the government offices, the new leaders sat behind desks that still smelled of British cologne. They signed papers with pens that had once belonged to colonial administrators. They spoke in English — the language of the oppressor — because their own tongues had never been taught the vocabulary of governance.

And in those first weeks, the mask slipped.

The Silence of the Forgotten

In the villages, life continued as it always had. The farmers woke before dawn and worked the same red soil their grandfathers had tilled. The fishermen cast their nets into Lake Victoria and hauled in tilapia that fed families but filled no national treasury.

They had been told independence would bring *change*. But the only change they saw was the color of the man who taxed them.
