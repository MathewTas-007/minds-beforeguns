Questions for Reflection

Zeinab taught African children to speak Portuguese, Arabic, and English‚Äîthe languages of their own oppression. Was this empowerment or collaboration? Can you resist a system by becoming fluent in its language, or does fluency imply acceptance?

She said "memory is not salvation, it's just knowing what you lost." But then she spent twenty-four years preserving memory. If memory doesn't save, why preserve it? What is the value of knowing what you've lost?

Zeinab was the last speaker of three languages. She documented them but couldn't keep them alive in sound. Is a language preserved in books still alive? Or is it a corpse dressed in grammar?

Kofi documented the slave trade in Portuguese‚Äîusing the oppressor's language to record the oppressor's crime. Is this strategic brilliance or ironic tragedy? Does documenting a crime in the criminal's language give it more or less legitimacy?

Zeinab died alone after twenty-four years without seeing her team. She chose this. She stayed when others left. Was this sacrifice noble or was it just loneliness dressed as mission? When does dedication become isolation?

She said "witnessing a death is its own form of life." But is witnessing enough? If you watch something die and document its death but cannot prevent it, have you honored it or just profited from its pain?

Her last words were "Remember everything. Forget nothing." But what is the purpose of total memory? Is it power? Is it burden? Is it vengeance? Is it love?

"I saw you, sister. I heard you. I remember you." And somewhere, in a courtyard in Kumasi, five hundred former students said the same. In thirty-three languages.
